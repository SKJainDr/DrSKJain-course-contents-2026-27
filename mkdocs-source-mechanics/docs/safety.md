# Mechanical Lab Safety, Precautions & Potential Harm

!!! warning "Essential Reading Before Any Real Mechanics Experiment"
    Every experiment in this lab that involves a real falling mass, spinning flywheel, stretched wire, swinging pendulum, or pressurized/rotating workshop rig carries real mechanical hazards. Read this section before touching any physical apparatus.

!!! danger "Warning"
    Never place your fingers, hands, or face in the path of a falling mass, a spinning flywheel, a whirling pendulum, or a wire under tension — stored mechanical energy releases suddenly and without warning if a support fails.

## Hazard Categories in This Lab

| Category | Typical source | Hazard level | Examples in this guide |
|---|---|---|---|
| Falling mass | Flywheel falling-mass method | Impact injury to feet/hands; crush hazard | Flywheel |
| Rotating machinery | Spinning flywheels, discs, rotors | Entanglement, impact from an unbalanced or uncontained rotor | Flywheel, Torsional Pendulum, Rotor Balancing Workshop |
| Wire under tension | Loaded wires (Searle's, static torsion) | Whiplash injury if a stressed wire snaps | Searle's Apparatus, Static Torsion |
| Swinging/whirling bodies | Pendulums, bifilar bars, Barton's pendulums | Impact injury if struck by a swinging mass | Bifilar Suspension, Compound Pendulum, Coupled/Forced Pendulums |

## Potential Harm

### Impact and Crush Injuries

Falling masses (as in the flywheel experiment), swinging pendulum bobs, and suddenly-released stored elastic energy (a snapped wire, an over-tensioned spring) can all cause significant impact injury if a person is struck. Heavy flywheels and rotor-balancing rigs additionally pose an entanglement risk if loose clothing, hair, or jewellery comes into contact with a spinning shaft.

### Wire and Spring Failure

Wires loaded close to or beyond their elastic limit (in Searle's apparatus or the static torsion experiment) can snap suddenly, releasing stored elastic energy as a rapid, whip-like recoil. Springs under significant tension or compression (coupling springs, isolator mounts) pose a similar, if usually smaller-scale, hazard if a hook or clamp gives way.

### Glassware and Fine-Bore Capillaries

The narrow-bore capillary tubes used in the viscosity and surface-tension experiments are fragile and can shatter under even modest mechanical stress, producing sharp fragments and, if the tube was filled with liquid, a spill of that liquid as well.

### Chemical Exposure

Test liquids such as glycerine are generally low-hazard but can still irritate skin and eyes on prolonged or repeated contact; any historical use of mercury (in barometric or manometric pressure-head arrangements) carries serious toxicity and specialized disposal requirements, and mercury-based apparatus should be handled only under appropriate institutional safety protocols, or preferably avoided altogether in favour of modern non-toxic alternatives.

### Electrical Hazards

The ultrasonic interferometer and any real ultrasonic NDT test equipment involve radio-frequency driver electronics; live circuitry should never be handled by students without supervision, and the equipment should always be switched off and unplugged before any physical adjustment beyond the intended micrometer/reflector control.

## General Precautions

- Read the full procedure for any real physical apparatus before switching it on or releasing any stored energy (falling mass, spinning flywheel, tensioned wire or spring).
- Keep hands, hair, loose clothing and jewellery well clear of any rotating or swinging component while it is in motion.
- Never exceed the rated load of a wire, beam, or spring; always load and unload gradually and watch for any sign of the elastic limit being approached (non-proportional strain, unusual sound, or visible deformation).
- Wear safety glasses when working with fine glassware, wires under tension, or any experiment where sudden mechanical failure could eject fragments or debris.
- Ensure rotating machinery (flywheels, rotor-balancing rigs) is properly guarded or contained, and never touch a spinning component directly.
- Treat all workshop rigs (structural load testing, rotor balancing) as capable of causing serious injury if operated outside their intended, supervised procedure — never attempt to exceed a rig's rated test load without explicit authorization.
- Report any damaged wire, cracked glassware, worn cord, or unusual vibration/noise to the instructor immediately rather than continuing to use the apparatus.
